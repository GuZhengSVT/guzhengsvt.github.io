PHYSICS

Physical Quantities

Internal Energy \( U \)

\[ \Delta U = Q + W \]

Here, \( Q \) is the heat absorbed by the system, and \( W \) is the work done by the system.

Entropy \( S \)

Entropy is a measure of the total energy in a system that is unavailable for work. As the total entropy increases, the system’s capacity to perform work decreases. Entropy serves as an indicator of energy degradation.

For a reversible process:

\[ \Delta S = \frac{Q}{T} \]

Here, \( Q \) is the heat absorbed by the system at a constant temperature \( T \) during the reversible process.

Enthalpy \( H \)

\[ H = U + pV \]

Helmholtz Free Energy \( F \)

\[ F = U - TS \]

The internal energy minus the energy unavailable for work equals the available (free) energy.

Gibbs Free Energy \( G \)

\[ G = F + pV = U - TS + pV = H - TS \]

Chemical Potential \( \mu \)

\[ \mu = \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial n} \right)_{T,p} = G_m \]

Where:

  • \( n \): Amount of substance (in moles)
  • \( G_m \): Molar Gibbs free energy

Equations

State Function Total Differentials (Reversible Processes)

Internal Energy Total Differential (Fundamental Thermodynamic Relation)

\[ dU = dQ + dW = TdS - pdV \]

Thus, \( U = U(S, V) \):

\[ \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial S} \right)_V = T, \quad \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_S = -p \]

Since the second partial derivatives of \( U \) are equal, we obtain the first Maxwell relation:

\[ \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial V} \right)_S = -\left( \frac{\partial p}{\partial S} \right)_V \]

Enthalpy Total Differential

\[ dH = dU + d(pV) = TdS - pdV + pdV + Vdp = TdS + Vdp \]

Helmholtz Free Energy Total Differential

\[ dF = -SdT - pdV \]

Gibbs Free Energy Total Differential

\[ dG = -SdT + Vdp \]

Entropy Total Differential

\[ dS = \frac{dU + pdV}{T} \]

Maxwell Relations

Maxwell Relations are crucial equations in thermodynamics that describe the relationships between state variables. The four Maxwell relations are:

  1. First Maxwell Relation:

    \[ \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial V} \right)_S = -\left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} \right)_V \]

  2. Second Maxwell Relation:

    \[ \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial P} \right)_S = \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \right)_P \]

  3. Third Maxwell Relation:

    \[ \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial V} \right)_T = \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial T} \right)_V \]

  4. Fourth Maxwell Relation:

    \[ \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial P} \right)_T = -\left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_P \]

Corollary:

\[ C_p - C_V = T \left( \frac{\partial p}{\partial T} \right)_V \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_p \]

For an ideal gas \( pV = nRT \), substituting yields:

\[ C_p - C_V = nR, \text{ a constant} \]

Equation of State

Definition: The functional relationship between temperature and state variables.

\[ f(p, V, T) = 0 \]

Ideal Gas Equation of State

\[ pV = nRT = NkT \]
  • \( n \): Amount of substance (moles)
  • \( R \): Universal gas constant
  • \( N \): Total number of particles
  • \( k \): Boltzmann constant

Van der Waals Equation

\[ \left( p + \frac{an^2}{V^2} \right)(V - nb) = nRT \]
  • \( a \) and \( b \): Constants

Common Coefficients

Volume Expansion Coefficient \( \alpha \)

\[ \alpha = \frac{1}{V} \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_p = \frac{1}{T}, \text{ for an ideal gas} \]

Pressure Coefficient \( \beta \)

\[ \beta = \frac{1}{p} \left( \frac{\partial p}{\partial T} \right)_V = \frac{1}{T}, \text{ for an ideal gas} \]

Isothermal Compressibility \( \kappa_T \)

\[ \kappa_T = -\frac{1}{V} \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial p} \right)_T = \frac{1}{p}, \text{ for an ideal gas} \]

Heat Capacity \( C \)

\[ C = \lim_{\Delta T \to 0} \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta T} \]

Constant Volume Heat Capacity \( C_V \)

\[ C_V = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V = T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_V \]

Constant Pressure Heat Capacity \( C_p \)

\[ C_p = \left( \frac{\partial H}{\partial T} \right)_p = T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_p \]

Polytropic Heat Capacity \( C_n \)

\[ C_n = T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_n \]

Ideal Gas Adiabatic Equation

\( \gamma \): [[#Adiabatic Coefficient]] \( C \): Constant

\[ pV^\gamma = C_1 \]

\[ TV^{\gamma-1} = C_2 \]

\[ P^{1-\gamma} T^\gamma = C_3 \]

Second Law of Thermodynamics

\[ dS \ge \frac{dQ}{T} \]
  1. Two adiabatic curves cannot intersect.
  2. \( T \): Temperature of the surroundings.
  3. For a reversible process, the surrounding temperature equals the system temperature \( T \).

Reversible Heat Engine Efficiency \( \eta \)

[[#Carnot’s Theorem]]

\[ \eta = 1 - \frac{T_1}{T_2} \]
  • \( T_2 \): Temperature of the high-temperature reservoir
  • \( T_1 \): Temperature of the low-temperature reservoir
  • \( 0 < \eta < 1 \)

Energy Equation in Throttling Process

For a throttling process, the energy balance equation is:

\[ H_1 = H_2 \]

Where \( H_1 \) and \( H_2 \) are the enthalpies before and after throttling, respectively.

Proof: The system is adiabatic, so \( \Delta U = W \).

\[ U_2 - U_1 = p_1 V_1 - p_2 V_2 \]

\[ U_2 + p_2 V_2 = U_1 + p_1 V_1 \]

\[ H_2 = H_1 \]

Joule-Thomson Coefficient

[[#Throttling Process]]

\[ \mu = \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial p} \right)_H = \frac{V}{C_p} (T \alpha - 1) \]

Fundamental Equation for Open Systems (Add \( \mu dn \))

Internal Energy Total Differential

\[ dU = dQ + dW + \mu dn = TdS - pdV + \mu dn \]

Enthalpy Total Differential

\[ dH = dU + d(pV) + \mu dn = TdS + Vdp + \mu dn \]

Helmholtz Free Energy Total Differential

\[ dF = -SdT - pdV + \mu dn \]

Gibbs Free Energy Total Differential

\[ dG = -SdT + Vdp + \mu dn \]

Grand Potential

\[ J = -pV \]

Clapeyron Equation

\[ \frac{dp}{dT} = \frac{S_m^\beta - S_m^\alpha}{V_m^\beta - V_m^\alpha} = \frac{L}{T(V_m^\beta - V_m^\alpha)} = \frac{s^\beta - s^\alpha}{v^\beta - v^\alpha} \]

Where:

  • \( L = T(S_m^\beta - S_m^\alpha) \): Latent heat of phase transition.
  • \( S_m \): Molar entropy.
  • \( s \): Specific entropy (entropy per unit mass).
  • \( v \): Specific volume (volume per unit mass).

Ehrenfest Equation

For [[#Second-Order Phase Transitions]], the Clapeyron equation becomes indeterminate \( \frac{0}{0} \). Applying L’Hôpital’s rule by taking partial derivatives with respect to \( T \) or \( p \) yields the Ehrenfest equations:

\[ \frac{dp}{dT} = \frac{\Delta c_p}{T v \Delta \alpha} \]

Alternatively, taking partial derivatives with respect to \( p \):

\[ \frac{dp}{dT} = \frac{\Delta \alpha}{\Delta \kappa_T} \]

First-Order Phase Transition Equations

Formula

For a first-order phase transition at the critical temperature \( T_c \), the Gibbs free energy \( G \) is continuous, but its first derivatives with respect to temperature and pressure are discontinuous:

  • \( \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial T} \right)_P = -S \)
  • \( \left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial P} \right)_T = V \)

At \( T_c \), these first derivatives exhibit jumps:

\[ \Delta S = S_2 - S_1 \neq 0 \]

\[ \Delta V = V_2 - V_1 \neq 0 \]

Here, \( S \) is entropy, \( V \) is volume, and subscripts 1 and 2 denote the phases before and after the transition.

Proof

First-order phase transitions are characterized by discontinuities in the first derivatives of thermodynamic potentials. For entropy:

  1. Continuity of Gibbs Free Energy: At \( T_c \), the Gibbs free energy \( G \) is continuous: \[ G_1 (T_c, P) = G_2 (T_c, P) \]
  2. Discontinuity of Entropy: Taking the partial derivative of \( G \) with respect to temperature gives the entropy: \[ S = -\left( \frac{\partial G}{\partial T} \right)_P \] At \( T_c \), the jump in entropy is: \[ \Delta S = S_2 - S_1 = -\left( \frac{\partial G_2}{\partial T} \right)_P + \left( \frac{\partial G_1}{\partial T} \right)_P \neq 0 \] This shows the discontinuity in entropy at the phase transition point.

Second-Order Phase Transition Equations

Formula

For a second-order phase transition at \( T_c \), the Gibbs free energy \( G \) and its first derivatives (entropy and volume) are continuous, but its second derivatives (heat capacity, compressibility, thermal expansion coefficient) are discontinuous:

  • \( \left( \frac{\partial^2 G}{\partial T^2} \right)_P = \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} = \frac{C_P}{T} \)
  • \( \left( \frac{\partial^2 G}{\partial P^2} \right)_T = \frac{\partial V}{\partial P} = -\kappa_T V \)

At \( T_c \), these second derivatives exhibit jumps:

\[ \Delta C_P = C_{P2} - C_{P1} \neq 0 \]

\[ \Delta \kappa_T = \kappa_{T2} - \kappa_{T1} \neq 0 \]

Here, \( C_P \) is the constant-pressure heat capacity, and \( \kappa_T \) is the isothermal compressibility.

Proof

Second-order phase transitions are characterized by discontinuities in the second derivatives of thermodynamic potentials. For heat capacity:

  1. Continuity of Entropy: At \( T_c \), the entropy \( S \) is continuous: \[ S_1 (T_c, P) = S_2 (T_c, P) \]
  2. Discontinuity of Heat Capacity: Taking the partial derivative of entropy with respect to temperature gives the heat capacity: \[ C_P = T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_P \] At \( T_c \), the jump in heat capacity is: \[ \Delta C_P = C_{P2} - C_{P1} = T \left( \left( \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial T} \right)_P - \left( \frac{\partial S_1}{\partial T} \right)_P \right) \neq 0 \] This shows the discontinuity in heat capacity at the phase transition point.

Gibbs Function Total Differential for Multicomponent Systems

\[ dG = -SdT + Vdp + \sum_i \mu_i dn_i \]

Gibbs Phase Rule

\[ f = k + 2 - \varphi \]

Where:

  • \( f \): Degrees of freedom (number of independent intensive variables).
  • \( k \): Number of components.
  • \( \varphi \): Number of phases.

de Broglie Relations

[[#de Broglie Waves]]

\[ \begin{cases} \varepsilon = \hbar \omega \\ \vec{p} = \hbar \vec{k} \end{cases} \]

Planck Constant

\[ \begin{align} h &= 6.626069934(89) \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \\ &= 4.135667662(25) \times 10^{-15} \, \text{eV} \cdot \text{s} \end{align} \]

Reduced Planck constant (Dirac constant):

\[ \hbar \equiv \frac{h}{2\pi} = 1.054571800(13) \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J} \cdot \text{s} \]

Uncertainty Principle

\[ \Delta q \Delta p \ge h \]

Or

\[ \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} \]

Generally, we take:

\[ \Delta q \Delta p \approx h \]

Quantum State Number for a 3D Free Particle

Temporarily ignoring quantum spin:

When particles move in a macroscopic container, their momentum and energy values are quasi-continuous.

Cartesian Momentum Representation

Find the number of quantum states for a free particle within volume \( V = L^3 \), in the momentum range \( p_x \) to \( p_x + dp_x \), \( p_y \) to \( p_y + dp_y \), \( p_z \) to \( p_z + dp_z \).

Solution 1: From the relation between momentum and quantum numbers for a [[#3D Free Particle]], the number of possible \( p_x \) states is:

\[ dn_x = \frac{L}{2\pi \hbar} dp_x \]

Similarly for the other directions:

\[ dn_x dn_y dn_z = \frac{V}{h^3} dp_x dp_y dp_z \]

Solution 2: From the definition of [[#Phase Cell]], the phase cell volume for a 3D free particle is \( \Delta q_1 \cdots \Delta q_r \Delta p_1 \cdots \Delta p_r \approx h^3 \). Here, the \( \mu \)-space volume is \( d\Omega = V dp_x dp_y dp_z \). The number of phase cells that fit into this volume gives the number of free particle quantum states:

\[ dn_x dn_y dn_z = \frac{d\Omega}{h^3} = \frac{V dp_x dp_y dp_z}{h^3} \]

Spherical Momentum Representation

\[ \displaylines{p_x = p \sin \theta \cos \varphi \\ p_y = p \sin \theta \sin \varphi \\ p_z = p \cos \theta} \]

\[ dp_x dp_y dp_z = p^2 \sin \theta \, dp \, d\theta \, d\varphi \]

Within volume \( V \), for momentum magnitude \( p \) to \( p + dp \), direction \( \theta \) to \( d\theta \), and \( \varphi \) to \( d\varphi \), the number of possible free particle states is:

\[ \frac{V p^2 \sin \theta \, dp \, d\theta \, d\varphi}{h^3} \]

There are three quantum numbers \( p, \theta, \varphi \), corresponding to three degrees of freedom.

Energy Representation

Integrating over \( \theta \) and \( \varphi \) (\( 0 < \theta < \pi, 0 < \varphi < 2\pi \)) gives:

\[ \frac{4\pi V p^2}{h^3} dp \]

Now, the momentum direction is arbitrary, and the degrees of freedom reduce to 1.

Since \( \varepsilon = \frac{p^2}{2m} \), within volume \( V \), the number of possible quantum states for a free particle with energy \( \varepsilon \) to \( \varepsilon + d\varepsilon \) is:

\[ D(\varepsilon) d\varepsilon = \frac{2\pi V}{h^3} (2m)^{3/2} \varepsilon^{1/2} d\varepsilon \]

Here, \( D(\varepsilon) \) is the density of states, representing the number of states per unit energy interval.

Including Quantum Spin

[[#Spin Angular Momentum]] The total number of quantum states = the above momentum quantum states × the number of possible spin states.

Example: For a particle with spin quantum number \( \frac{1}{2} \), \( S_z = 2s + 1 = 2 \), so the above result should be multiplied by 2.

PHYSICS

Fundamentals of Hydrostatics

Properties of Gases

Compressibility

Bulk Modulus

Definition: The pressure increase required to produce a unit relative volume change.

$$ E=-\frac{dp}{dV/V} $$

For a fixed mass of gas, volume is inversely proportional to density, i.e.,

$$ \frac{d \rho}{\rho}=-\frac{dV}{V} $$

Substituting back gives:

$$ E=\rho \frac{dp}{d \rho} $$

The bulk modulus of water at room temperature: $2.1 \times 10^9 \, \text{N/m}^2$.

Under normal conditions, water can be considered an incompressible fluid.

Viscosity

Newton’s Law of Viscosity

The frictional resistance generated by fluid motion is proportional to the contact area.

$$ \tau =\mu \frac{du}{d \vec{n}} $$

$\tau$: Frictional stress, the frictional force per unit area.

$\vec{n}$: Normal direction of the contact surface.

$\mu$: Proportionality constant, called the viscosity coefficient of the fluid, with units of $N \cdot s/m^2$.

$\frac{du}{d \vec{n}}$: Velocity gradient.

The viscosity coefficient varies for different fluid media and changes with temperature but is largely independent of pressure.

The viscosity coefficient of gases increases with temperature.

Sutherland’s Formula

An approximate formula describing how the viscosity coefficient of air changes with temperature:

$$ \frac{\mu}{\mu_0}=\left(\frac{T}{288.15}\right)^{1.5}\frac{288.15+C}{T+C} $$

$\mu_0$: Viscosity coefficient of air at $288.15 \, \text{K}$.

$C$: Constant, with a value of $110.4 \, \text{K}$.

Kinematic Viscosity Coefficient

$$ \nu=\frac{\mu}{\rho} $$

$\nu$: Kinematic viscosity coefficient, with units of $m^2/s$.

$\mu$: Viscosity coefficient.

$\rho$: Density.

Thermal Conductivity

Definition: When a temperature gradient exists in a gas along a certain direction, heat will transfer from the higher-temperature region to the lower-temperature region. This property is called the thermal conductivity of the gas.

The heat transferred per unit time is proportional to the heat transfer area and the temperature gradient along the heat flow direction:

$$ q=-\lambda \frac{\partial T}{\partial \vec{n}} $$

$q$: Heat per unit time through a unit area, with units $kJ/(m^2 \cdot s)$.

$\frac{\partial T}{\partial \vec{n}}$: Temperature gradient, with units $K/m$.

$\lambda$: Thermal conductivity coefficient, with units $kJ/(m \cdot K \cdot s)$.

The negative sign indicates that the direction of heat transfer is always opposite to the temperature gradient.

Classification of Fluids

Continuum Hypothesis

Ideal Fluid

A model that ignores viscosity. In this model, fluid particles do not experience viscous forces. Commonly used for gases.

A gas that ignores viscosity is called an ideal gas.

Pressure Isotropy

In an ideal fluid, the pressure at a point is independent of the orientation of the surface and is only a continuous function of spatial coordinates.

Incompressible Fluid

A model that ignores compressibility or elasticity, treating the bulk modulus as infinite or the fluid density as constant. Commonly used for liquids.

Solving the flow laws of incompressible fluids only requires adherence to mechanical laws, without considering thermodynamic relationships.

For gases at low flow velocities, they can also be treated as incompressible fluids.

Adiabatic Fluid

A model that ignores thermal conductivity, treating the thermal conductivity coefficient as zero. The thermal conductivity of low-speed air is generally very small and can be considered adiabatic.

A gas model that ignores heat conduction between gas particles is called an adiabatic gas.

Perfect Gas

In any state, there is a functional relationship between the pressure, density, and temperature of a gas:

$$ p=p(\rho,T) $$

Equation of State for a Perfect Gas

$$ p=\frac{\overline{R}}{m}\rho T $$

$\overline{R}$: Universal gas constant, $8315 \, m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)$.

$m$: Relative molecular mass of a specific gas.

When $R=\frac{\overline{R}}{m}$,

$$ p=\rho R T $$

$R$ is the gas constant. For air, it is approximately $287.035 \, m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)$.

Hydrostatic Equilibrium Equation

In a static fluid, consider a point $P$ with pressure $p$.

Construct a Cartesian coordinate system where the pressure at each point in the fluid is:

$$ p(x,y,z) $$

Construct a rectangular parallelepiped centered at $P$ with edges parallel to the coordinate axes and lengths $dx, dy, dz$.

For the $x$-axis direction, the forces on the two faces are:

$$ \left[p(x_0,y_0,z_0)+\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right)\left(\frac{dx}{2}\right)\right]dy \, dz $$$$ \left[p(x_0,y_0,z_0)-\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right)\left(\frac{dx}{2}\right)\right]dy \, dz $$

The body force on the fluid particle in the $x$-axis direction is:

$$ f_x \rho \, dx \, dy \, dz $$

$f_x$ is the component of the body force per unit mass in the $x$-axis direction.

Since the fluid is static, the forces on the fluid particle are balanced.

The force balance equation in the $x$-axis direction is:

$$ \left[p(x_0,y_0,z_0)-\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right)\left(\frac{dx}{2}\right)\right]dy \, dz - \left[p(x_0,y_0,z_0)+\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right)\left(\frac{dx}{2}\right)\right]dy \, dz + f_x \rho \, dx \, dy \, dz = 0 $$

Simplifying gives:

$$ \frac{\partial p}{\partial x}=\rho f_x $$

Similarly:

$$ \frac{\partial p}{\partial y}=\rho f_y $$$$ \frac{\partial p}{\partial z}=\rho f_z $$

The total differential of $p$ is:

$$ dp=\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial p}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial p}{\partial z}dz $$

Thus:

$$ dp=\rho(f_x dx + f_y dy + f_z dz) $$

Define the body force potential function:

$$ \varOmega=\varOmega(x,y,z) $$

Its total differential is:

$$ d \varOmega=\frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial x}dx + \frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial y}dy + \frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial z}dz $$

Where $\frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial x}=f_x$, $\frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial y}=f_y$, $\frac{\partial \varOmega}{\partial z}=-f_z$.

From these relationships, we get:

$$ dp=-\rho d \varOmega $$

Integrating both sides over $x, y, z$ gives:

$$ p=-\rho \varOmega + C \text{ (constant)} $$$$ C=p + \rho \varOmega $$

If the pressure $p_a$ at a known point A, the difference in body force potential $\varOmega_a - \varOmega$, and the density $\rho$ of the static fluid (uniform everywhere) are known, the pressure at any point can be determined from its body force potential $\varOmega$:

$$ p=p_a + \rho (\varOmega_a - \varOmega) $$

Corollary: An isobaric surface in the fluid must also be an equipotential surface of the body force.

Atmosphere

Atmospheric Layers

Lower Atmosphere

  • Altitude: Sea level to 85 km
  • Characteristics: Uniform composition, nitrogen accounts for 78.1% of the volume, oxygen accounts for 21%

Troposphere

  • Altitude:
    • Equator: 16–18 km
    • Mid-latitudes: 10–12 km
    • Poles: 7–10 km
  • Mass: 75% of the total atmospheric mass
  • Characteristics: Vertical air movements, storms, and thunderstorms. Temperature decreases rapidly with altitude.

Tropopause

A transition layer, several hundred meters to 1–2 km thick.

Stratosphere

  • Altitude: Troposphere to 32 km
  • Mass: About one-quarter of the atmospheric mass
  • Characteristics: No weather phenomena, horizontal air flow, constant temperature (average about 216.65 K)

Middle Atmosphere

  • Altitude: 32–85 km
  • Mass: 1/3000 of the total
  • Temperature: First increases then decreases, dropping below 106 K at 85 km.

Upper Atmosphere

  • Altitude: Above 85 km
  • Characteristics: Non-uniform composition, direct absorption of solar radiation

Thermosphere

  • Altitude: 85–500 km
  • Temperature: Increases with altitude, reaching up to 1370 K at 500 km during the day.
  • Characteristics: Directly affected by short-wave solar radiation.

Exosphere

  • Altitude: Above 500 km, gradually merging with interstellar space
  • Mass: $1/10^{11}$ of the total
  • Characteristics: Too rarefied for temperature definition. Air molecules can escape into space.

Upper Atmosphere and Ionosphere

  • The upper atmosphere is dissociated into electrons and ions by solar short-wave radiation, forming the ionosphere.
  • Above 100 km, the air is a good conductor.
  • Above 150 km, the air is too thin to transmit sound.

D Layer

  • Altitude: 60–80 km

E Layer

  • Altitude: 100–120 km

F1 Layer

  • Altitude: 180–220 km

F2 Layer

  • Altitude: 300–350 km

International Standard Atmosphere

A standardized set of atmospheric parameters (pressure, density, temperature, etc.) for aeronautical engineering, based on statistical averages for mid-latitude conditions, known as the International Standard Atmosphere.

Fundamentals of Fluid Kinematics and Dynamics

Flow Field

Flow field: The space filled with moving fluid.

Flow parameters: Physical quantities that describe the motion characteristics of the fluid, such as velocity, density, and pressure.

Fluid mechanics methods: Lagrangian method, Eulerian method.

Lagrangian Method

Focuses on particles (motion).

  • Studies how the motion parameters of fluid particles change over time and their trajectories.
  • Combines the changes in motion parameters of all fluid particles to derive the overall flow field behavior.

Eulerian Method

Focuses on spatial points (stationary).

  • Studies how the motion parameters of fluid particles change over time as they pass through fixed points in space.
  • Combines the changes in motion parameters at all spatial points to derive the overall flow field behavior.

In the Eulerian method, flow field parameters are generally functions of spatial coordinates and time.

For velocity:

$$ v=v(x,y,z,t) $$

The four variables are independent.

In a 3D space, a Cartesian coordinate system is typically used, and scalar parameters are decomposed into $x, y, z$ components for analysis.

$$ v_x=v_x(x,y,z,t) $$

The acceleration component is obtained by differentiation:

$$ a_x=\frac{d v_x}{dt}=\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt} $$

Since $\frac{dx}{dt}=v_x$, $\frac{dy}{dt}=v_y$, $\frac{dz}{dt}=v_z$,

$$ a_x=\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial t} + v_x \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + v_y \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} + v_z \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial z} $$

Thus, acceleration is a function of time and position:

$$ a_x=a_x(t,x,y,z) $$

Local Acceleration

The first term on the right represents the rate of change of fluid particle velocity at a fixed point in space over time, called the local acceleration (relationship between velocity and time).

Local acceleration arises from the time-dependent variation of velocity in the flow field.

Convective Acceleration

The last three terms reflect the rate of change of velocity as a fluid particle moves from one point to an adjacent point along the velocity vector direction at the same instant, called the convective acceleration (relationship between velocity and displacement).

Convective acceleration arises from the non-uniformity of the flow field.

Unsteady Flow Field

A flow field where at least one spatial point has physical quantities that change over time.

Steady Flow Field

A flow field where the physical quantities at any spatial point do not change over time.

Pathline

The set of all spatial points traversed by a marked fluid particle over a period of time is called the pathline of that particle.

Streamline

A curve in the flow field that is tangent to the velocity vector at every point is called a streamline.

A streamline is a curve formed by different fluid particles at the same instant, indicating the direction of velocity for different particles at that moment.

Characteristics:

  • In a steady flow field, streamlines do not change over time.

  • An unsteady flow field has streamlines that change with time.

  • In a steady flow field, the streamline passing through a spatial point coincides with the pathline of all fluid particles passing through that point.

  • Generally, streamlines do not intersect (at the same instant and point, there cannot be two velocity directions).

  • At points where velocity is zero, streamlines can intersect. Such points are usually called stagnation points.

  • At points where velocity is infinite, streamlines can intersect. Such points are usually called singular points.

  • Streamlines can be tangent, and after the point of tangency, the two lines coincide.

  • Every point in the flow field has a streamline passing through it. The collection of all streamlines is called the streamline spectrum or simply flow pattern.

Streamline Differential Equation

Consider a point $M(x,y,z)$ on a streamline with velocity $\vec{v}$. The infinitesimal segment of the streamline at $M$ has length $ds$, decomposed into $v_x, v_y, v_z$ and $dx, dy, dz$ in Cartesian coordinates.

The velocity direction at any point on the streamline is the same as the tangent direction of the streamline, so:

$$ \cos(\vec{v},\vec{i})=\frac{v_x}{v}=\frac{dx}{ds} $$

$\vec{i}$ is the unit vector in the $x$-axis direction, and similarly for $y$ and $z$.

$$ \frac{dx}{v_x}=\frac{dy}{v_y}=\frac{dz}{v_z} $$

This is the differential equation of streamlines.

Given the velocity distribution, the shape of the streamline passing through any point in the flow field can be determined.

Stream Tube

In the flow field, a closed curve $C$ that is not a streamline. Drawing streamlines through every point on $C$ forms a tubular surface called a stream tube.

Two-Dimensional Analysis

![Pasted image 20240902212258.png][1]

Consider a rectangular fluid particle ABCD in the flow field with side lengths $\delta_x$ and $\delta_y$, both small.

Let $v_x, v_y$ be the velocity components at point A, both continuous functions of spatial coordinates. The velocities at points B and D can be expressed using Taylor series expansions around A.

Since the side lengths of the fluid particle are very small, higher-order terms can be neglected.

$$ v_{Bx}=v_x + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\delta_x $$$$ v_{By}=v_y + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x}\delta_x $$

As the fluid particle moves, point B moves relative to point A.

The relative velocity in the $x$-direction is $v_{Bx} - v_x = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\delta_x$, and similarly for the $y$-direction.

The relative velocity of D with respect to A is $v_{Dx} - v_x = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\delta_y$, $v_{Dy} - v_y = \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y}\delta_y$.

Linear Deformation Motion

![Pasted image 20240902212840.png][2]

The relative velocities $\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\delta_x$ and $\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y}\delta_y$ represent the linear deformation rates of the sides of rectangle ABCD. Over time $dt$,

$$ AB'=AB + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x}\delta_x \, dt $$$$ AD'=AD + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y}\delta_y \, dt $$

The relative change in area is:

$$ \frac{d(\delta S)}{\delta S \cdot dt}=\frac{AB' \cdot CD' - AB \cdot CD}{AB \cdot CD \cdot dt} $$

Neglecting higher-order terms:

$$ \frac{d(\delta S)}{\delta S \cdot dt}=\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} $$

Extending to 3D space:

$$ \frac{d(\delta V)}{\delta V \cdot dt}=\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} $$

Angular Deformation Motion

![Pasted image 20240902214219.png][3]

The relative velocities $\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x}\delta x$ and $\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\delta y$ represent the rotation of sides AB and AD about point A.

Defining counterclockwise rotation as positive:

The angular velocity of AB:

$$ \frac{d\alpha_1}{dt}=\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x}\delta_x / \delta_x=\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x} $$

Similarly, the angular velocity of AD:

$$ \frac{d \alpha_2}{dt}=-\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} $$
